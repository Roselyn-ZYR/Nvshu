Nüshu (女书)
## Background

Nüshu is currently the only known script in the world created, used, and transmitted exclusively by women. Originating from Jiangyong County in Hunan Province, China, it emerged during the feudal era when women were denied access to formal education. Despite this exclusion, they created and learned Nüshu to record their life stories and exchange sentiments with their "Laotong" (sworn sisters). They expressed themselves in spaces often overlooked by the male gaze—embroidered on textiles, written on fans, or simply scratched into the earth with wooden sticks. Research into Nüshu reveals the multitude of injustices women endured, as well as the profound spiritual solidarity they shared.

## Function: A Bridge of Languages & Empowerment

This digital archive serves as a contemporary vessel for a script that was once nearly lost to history. It is designed not just as a static database, but as an interactive experience of discovery:

Trilingual Translation: Every character is meticulously translated into Chinese, English, and German, bridging the rural origins of the script with the global academic discourse at Bauhaus-Universität Weimar.

Contextual Application: Beyond literal meanings, each character is accompanied by a living sentence, demonstrating how this "her-story" can be spoken in today’s world.

The "Blind Box" of Creativity: When a user clicks a character, it triggers a "Blind Box" revelation. Instead of a mere definition, you will discover a female artist—ranging from historical pioneers to contemporary visionaries.

Empowerment through Visibility: This feature is a tribute to the "invisible" creators of Nüshu. By linking ancient characters to modern female artists, we celebrate female power, creativity, and infinite possibilities, ensuring their voices are no longer scratched into the earth, but etched into the digital future.
